# Lesson 07 — Sample Analysis Mode

**Duration:** 12 minutes
**Outcome:** You upload any audio file, get a full breakdown, and walk away with a Suno prompt that recreates the vibe.
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 04 to 06 complete.

---

## Context (for the LMS module page)

Sample Analysis Mode is the most technically powerful feature in Suno Writer. You upload any track and the app runs a six-layer Librosa analysis (key, BPM, chord progression, energy map, tonal character, groove, structure) and then layers Gemini's qualitative listening on top. The output is a full sonic breakdown plus a ready-to-paste Suno prompt that recreates the vibe. Artist names get auto-scrubbed to sonic descriptions, which protects you legally and forces you to think in sound, not labels.

## What You Will Learn
- What gets analyzed and how
- The six-layer Librosa pipeline in plain English
- How Gemini's qualitative listening adds context
- How to use the analysis to recreate vs reference a vibe
- Why artist names get scrubbed and why that protects you
- How to combine Sample Analysis with Songwriting or Instrumental Mode

---

## Step-by-Step Guide

1. Click Sample Analysis Mode.
2. Click the upload button or drag an audio file directly into the chat area.
3. Wait for the analysis to complete (usually under 30 seconds).
4. Read the breakdown: key, BPM, chord progression, energy curve, tonal character, groove, structure.
5. Scroll to the generated Style Prompt and Lyric Prompt at the bottom.
6. Notice that any artist references in the analysis are translated to sonic descriptions instead of artist names.
7. Copy both prompts into Suno to recreate the vibe.
8. (Bonus) Switch back to Songwriting Mode and use the analysis as a reference for a brand new song in the same sonic family.

## Screen Recording Script

### [00:00 — 00:30] Cold open

**[ON SCREEN]** Sample Analysis Mode active. Empty upload area.

**Narration:**
"Sample Analysis Mode. Upload any track and the app tells you exactly what makes it tick, then hands you a Suno prompt that recreates the vibe. This is the lesson where the app stops feeling like a tool and starts feeling like a producer in the room with you."

### [00:30 — 01:30] What gets analyzed

**[ON SCREEN]** Pull up a graphic listing the six layers.

**Narration:**
"Six things get analyzed. Key, BPM, chord progression, energy map across the song, tonal character, and groove. That is the technical layer. On top of that, the app does a qualitative listen and tells you what the song actually feels like. Numbers plus vibe."

### [01:30 — 03:00] The six-layer pipeline in plain English

**[ON SCREEN]** Animated graphic of each layer firing in sequence.

**Narration:**
"Quick plain English on the six layers. HPSS separates the harmonic content from the percussive content so the analyzer can hear melody and drums separately. The chord layer detects the actual chord progression. The melody layer pulls out the lead line. The groove layer measures swing and timing. The timbre layer figures out the tonal character, like is this warm or bright or distorted. And the structure layer maps the energy across the whole song so you can see exactly where the drops and bridges are. All of that runs in seconds."

### [03:00 — 06:00] Live upload

**[ON SCREEN]** Drag an audio file into the upload area. Watch the analysis run. Scroll through the output.

**Narration:**
"I am going to drag in a track right now. Watch. Analysis runs. Here is the breakdown. Key of F minor. 87 BPM. Chord progression is a four-chord loop. Energy map shows two drops, one at the one minute mark, one at the two minute mark. Tonal character is warm, slightly distorted, vintage. Groove is laid back, slightly behind the beat."

### [06:00 — 07:30] Why artist names get scrubbed

**[ON SCREEN]** Highlight a section where an artist name would have appeared but was replaced with a sonic description.

**Narration:**
"Notice something. Nowhere in this output does it say sounds like artist X. The system auto-scrubs artist names and replaces them with sonic descriptions. This protects you in two ways. One, Suno itself filters out artist names so prompts that name artists fail. Two, when you describe a sound instead of a person, you understand the sound on a much deeper level. You stop chasing names and start chasing tones."

### [07:30 — 10:00] The recreate prompt

**[ON SCREEN]** Scroll to the generated Style and Lyric Prompts. Highlight both.

**Narration:**
"At the bottom of the analysis you get a recreate prompt. Style Prompt and Lyric Prompt, ready to paste into Suno. This is not a copy of the original. It is a translation of the vibe into a Suno-friendly instruction set. Paste it. Generate. You will get a brand new song that lives in the same sonic family as the one you uploaded."

### [10:00 — 11:30] Recreate vs reference

**[ON SCREEN]** Switch to Songwriting Mode. Reference the analysis in a new prompt.

**Narration:**
"Two ways to use the analysis. Recreate, where you paste the prompt straight into Suno and get a track in the same family. Or reference, where you take the analysis back to Songwriting Mode and use it as creative direction for a brand new song with your own lyrics. Same vibe, your story."

### [11:30 — 12:00] Close

**Narration:**
"Homework, analyze a song you wish you had produced, generate the prompt, and tell us in the community what surprised you about the breakdown. Next lesson we get into the Arrange view."
